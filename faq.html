About this site

Every two years, the Washington state legislature adopts an operating budget, funded by revenue from sales, property, business, and other taxes like those on liquor and tobacco. The operating budget funds Washington's public schools and universities, hospitals, prisons, courts, health care, and other critical state services.

This visualization opens up Washington’s budget for everyone – so it is easy to see how much is spent, on what, and how it compares to previous years. The charts display “Real” data, which are adjusted for inflation, alongside “Nominal” data, which are not. In this way, it is easy to compare real year-over-year expenditures on budget items as broad as Public Education, and as specific as the Department of Fish and Wildlife.

Where do state revenues come from?

State funding for the operating budget is primarily derived from three sources: sales taxes on most goods, construction and repair services; businesses taxes, including the Business & Occupation (B&O) tax and Public Utilities Tax (PUT); and property taxes, which mostly fund services in local communities, though about 20% goes to the state to support public schools. Read our Washington State Budget 101 Guide here »

This site illustrates Washington state’s operating expenses as represented in the "Near General Fund - State" (NGFS), a commonly used measure of state spending. The NGFS includes the following funds and accounts:

  • General Fund
  • Equal Justice Subaccount
  • Education Legacy Trust Account
  • Health Services Account
  • Pension Funding Stabilization Account
  • Public Safety and Education Account
  • Student Achievement Fund
  • Violence Reduction and Drug Enforcement Account
  • Water Quality Account

Note that Washington also receives additional funding (not shown here) from the federal government that is reserved for specific services, such as Food Stamps, Medicaid, and children’s health. The state legislature also adopts separate budgets for transportation (using the gas tax and other dedicated revenue) and capital projects.

Data sources

All charts and tables display both “real” (inflation-adjusted) budget numbers calculated by the Economic Opportunity Institute, alongside “nominal” (unadjusted) numbers provided by the Washington State Office of Fiscal Management. Amounts prior to FY2012 were updated January 2013; the 2011-13 amounts reflect the Enacted budgets from the 2012 legislative session. All "real" (inflation-adjusted) amounts are shown in 2012 dollars.
